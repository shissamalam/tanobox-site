# TanoBox business plan: research findings across 10 key areas

**A one-time-purchase, self-hosted financial modeling tool for small AEC firms occupies a genuinely uncontested market position.** No existing FP&A software combines perpetual licensing, self-hosted deployment, and AEC-industry focus — every competitor is SaaS-subscription and industry-agnostic. The US SMB FP&A market is estimated at **$500M–$800M**, with **75% of architecture firms employing fewer than 10 people** and most relying on QuickBooks plus Excel for financial management. The timing aligns with a measurable anti-SaaS sentiment (37signals' ONCE movement, Satya Nadella's "SaaS is dead" declaration) and strong hardware-appliance precedents from pfSense, Firewalla, and Synology. Below are detailed findings across all 10 research areas.

## 1. The SMB FP&A market is large, growing fast, and underserved at the bottom

The global FP&A software market was valued at **$3.9–$5.8 billion in 2024**, depending on the research firm (Verified Market Research, Dataintelo), with projected growth to **$9.7–$13.9 billion by 2031–2033** at a **10–17% CAGR**. North America accounts for roughly **37% of global revenues**, implying a US FP&A market of approximately **$1.4–$2.2 billion**. The SMB segment (under 50 employees) represents an estimated **$500M–$800M** of this, based on SMBs comprising 30–40% of total software spend in the category.

The target verticals are substantial in scale. The US has approximately **19,000–20,000 architecture firms** (AIA 2024 Firm Survey), **860,000+ professional services firms** (Census Bureau NAICS 54, 2020), and **749,000–1 million+ restaurants** (National Restaurant Association, 2024). Architecture and engineering services alone account for **117,056 establishments** generating industry revenue of roughly **$55 billion** (IBISWorld).

The adoption gap is TanoBox's core opportunity. Among businesses with **1–19 employees**, **30% still rely primarily on Excel or Google Sheets** for financial management, and **21% use no software at all** (SMB Group, 2022). Only **54% of all SMBs** use dedicated planning, budgeting, or forecasting software beyond spreadsheets. The top reason for non-adoption is telling: **50% say "manual methods/spreadsheets have worked well for us."** Meanwhile, **87% of SMBs** are considering integrated financial software within the next 12–24 months (SMB Group/BILL, 2025). SMB IT spending is growing at **9.8% in 2025** (Gartner), with **62% of SMBs increasing IT budgets** — the highest net-increase since 2021.

## 3. Small architecture firms are the ideal beachhead — here's why

AIA has **101,000+ individual members** across **200+ chapters** with a **90% renewal rate** and **72% of members having dues paid by employers**. There are approximately **120,000 licensed architects in the US** (NCARB, 2024). The AIA Firm Survey identifies **more than 19,000 architecture firms**, with a highly concentrated size distribution:

- **28%** are sole practitioners
- **32%** have 2–4 employees
- **15%** have 5–9 employees
- **75% of all firms have fewer than 10 employees**

The financial profile of these firms is compelling. Operating profit on net revenue reached **21.4%** in 2024 — a 10-year high (Deltek Clarity, 2025). Net revenue per employee rose **11% year-over-year**, and firms forecast **9.6% net revenue growth** for 2025. However, billings from smaller firms **fell 50% between 2015 and 2023**, while large-firm billings grew 40%, suggesting small firms face growing financial pressure that modeling tools could address.

Technology adoption in small firms lags significantly. Only **27% of small firms** use AI in day-to-day work versus **61% of large firms** (AIA 2024). The Deltek Clarity Report notes a persistent **"gap between intent and full integration"** — many firms remain heavily reliant on manual processes for accounting and resource management. The typical small firm tech stack is **QuickBooks + Excel + Autodesk design tools**, possibly with Monograph ($25–$300/mo) for project management. Purpose-built AEC financial tools like **Deltek Ajera** and **BQE CORE** target mid-to-large firms with enterprise pricing.

**The software spend data confirms the opportunity.** Architecture firms routinely invest in design software (AutoCAD/Revit subscriptions run $2,000+/yr), demonstrating willingness to pay for specialized tools. But their financial management remains primitive — a clear mismatch between design sophistication and business management capability.

## 4. The QuickBooks ecosystem has a 3-statement modeling gap worth exploiting

**QuickBooks dominates small business accounting with 7+ million users worldwide** and approximately **4.9 million US-based businesses** (DataCaptive, 2025). Its market share is **62% in overall US accounting software** and **80–84% in the small-business segment** specifically. Of those users, **62% are small businesses under 50 employees**, and **83% have revenue under $50 million**.

The QuickBooks App Store has **600+ apps across ~25 categories** but **no dedicated "financial modeling" or "FP&A" category**. While apps like Jirav, Fathom, LivePlan, Calxa, and Clockwork offer partial forecasting capabilities, **no single dominant app provides full 3-statement financial modeling** purpose-built for SMBs. The market is fragmented — at least 12+ competing apps without clear leadership — which signals demand without a winner.

Intuit's new **App Partner Program** (launched July 28, 2025) restructured developer economics significantly. The **Builder tier is free** with 500,000 CorePlus API calls/month. Critically, **Intuit does not take a revenue share** — developers keep 100% of their app revenue and pay only tiered platform fees. For a new app starting out, the free Builder tier is sufficient. Silver ($300–$500/mo) provides more API capacity when scaling.

For a self-hosted desktop product connecting to QuickBooks Online, the OAuth 2.0 requirement creates a wrinkle: **production redirect URIs must use HTTPS** (no localhost). The standard workaround is a lightweight cloud callback endpoint that relays tokens to the desktop app. For **QuickBooks Desktop integration**, the architecture is entirely different — SOAP/qbXML via the Web Connector, which **requires no Intuit approval** and no OAuth. Given the ~3.7M remaining Desktop users, this is a viable secondary integration path.

## 5. Hardware-bundled software has strong precedent and favorable economics

The software-on-hardware delivery model is well-proven across multiple B2B categories:

- **Netgate/pfSense**: Sells hardware appliances ($189–$1,000+) with pre-installed pfSense Plus software. **13 million+ installations worldwide.** One-time purchase includes lifetime software updates. Used by the US Navy and enterprise customers.
- **Firewalla**: Hardware firewalls ($130–$550) with **zero monthly fees**. All software included with purchase.
- **Synology**: ~$184.5M revenue from NAS hardware with free proprietary DiskStation Manager OS. Hardware-as-delivery-vehicle model sustains a multi-hundred-million-dollar business.
- **Home Assistant Green/Yellow**: ~$99–$150 plug-and-play appliance with optional $6.50/mo cloud subscription.
- **Henry Schein One/TechCentral**: Ships pre-configured "dental network in a box" — servers, workstations, and software bundled for dental practices. Direct SMB-professional parallel to TanoBox.

Hardware economics are favorable for bundling. **Intel N100 mini PCs** wholesale from Shenzhen manufacturers at **$90–$130** (16GB/512GB configuration, 50–100 unit MOQ) versus **$170–$190 retail**. Key SKUs include the Beelink EQ12 (dual 2.5GbE, <8W idle), MinisForum UN100, and Lenovo ThinkCentre Tiny (business-class). At wholesale cost of ~$100–$120, bundling into a **$499–$799 total package** yields **$380–$680 effective software revenue per unit** with hardware at near-cost.

The model works best when the customer perceives they're buying a **solution, not a computer** — and when the target market values **plug-and-play simplicity** over DIY configuration. Both conditions apply to architecture firm principals who spend their time designing buildings, not configuring servers.

## 6. Perpetual licensing is having a genuine moment — price it at $499–$999

The anti-SaaS movement gained serious momentum in 2024–2025. **37signals launched ONCE** (once.com) with the explicit mission to "help lead the way out" of SaaS, offering self-hosted tools as one-time purchases. **Satya Nadella declared "SaaS is dead"** on the BG2 podcast in December 2024. IDC predicts that by 2028, **70% of software vendors will refactor pricing** away from pure seat-based models. The Broadcom/VMware forced subscription transition generated severe customer backlash, further fueling demand for ownership models.

Pricing research points to clear sweet spots. For B2B perpetual-license software, the traditional model adds **annual maintenance at 18–22% of list price** (Revenera/Flexera data), meaning a $1,000 license generates ~$200/year in recurring revenue. **SMBs often have discretionary purchase authority up to $500–$1,000** without committee approval (ProfitWell). The **$299–$499 range** allows individual decision-makers to buy without procurement friction; **above $1,000** adds friction but signals seriousness.

The anchoring math is straightforward: if competing SaaS costs **$50–$100/month ($600–$1,200/year)**, a one-time price of **$499–$999** represents a compelling deal at the 1–2 year payback threshold. Bundled with hardware at **$699–$1,299**, the total price anchors against years of subscription savings. JetBrains' perpetual fallback license (perpetual access after 12 months of subscription) and Sendy's $69 one-time email tool demonstrate that developer-oriented products can thrive without subscriptions.

AppSumo lifetime deals typically run **$39–$149** and generate high volume but attract deal-seekers — **~40% of lifetime deals fail within 3 years** because the economics don't sustain development. For TanoBox, AppSumo could serve as a validation/awareness play but should not be the primary channel. The audience (marketers and generalist entrepreneurs) is poorly matched to AEC professionals.

## 8. Building the calculation engine: DAG architecture with iterative circular-reference solving

The recommended architecture for a custom financial calculation engine is a **Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) with topological sorting** — the same pattern underlying every modern spreadsheet engine. The core components are:

**A cell/node registry** where each calculable financial value (revenue, COGS, net income) is a node with a formula function and current value. **A dependency graph** with directed edges from dependencies to dependents. **Topological sort** (Kahn's algorithm, O(V+E) complexity) to determine evaluation order. **Dirty tracking** for incremental recalculation — when one input changes, only downstream nodes recompute.

For the three-statement model, the key linkages that the engine must handle are: **Net Income** flows from the income statement bottom line → cash flow statement top line → balance sheet retained earnings. **Depreciation** appears as an IS expense, a CFS operating add-back, and reduces BS PP&E. **Working capital changes** (AR, AP, inventory) connect BS current assets/liabilities to CFS operating adjustments. **Ending cash** from the CFS flows to the BS cash line.

The critical implementation challenge is **circular references**, which are inherent in 3-statement models. Interest expense depends on average debt balance, which depends on cash flow, which depends on net income, which depends on interest expense. The recommended approach is **iterative convergence**: run the full calculation loop 10–30 times until values converge within a $1 tolerance. A simpler alternative is a **circuit-breaker toggle** that uses beginning-of-period (non-circular) balances rather than average balances, which is standard Wall Street practice for simplicity.

Key TypeScript libraries to use alongside the custom engine include **`formula.js`** (400+ Excel formula implementations including PV, FV, NPV, IRR, PMT), **`decimal.js`** (arbitrary-precision arithmetic — critical for financial accuracy, never use floating-point), **`toposort`** (topological sort for DAGs), and **`financial`** (zero-dependency TVM functions matching numpy-financial). For the reactive UI layer, the **Signals pattern** (used in SolidJS/Angular) maps naturally to financial modeling where computed values auto-update when dependencies change.

The PostgreSQL schema should use **`NUMERIC(20,4)`** for all financial values (never FLOAT), with composite indexes on `(scenario_id, line_item_id, period_id)`. A typical 3-statement model with ~100 line items × 20 periods × 3 scenarios = 6,000 cells — easily computable in-memory in under 1ms, requiring no exotic database optimization.

## 10. Legal foundations: Texas LLC, financial disclaimers, and QuickBooks API compliance

**Texas LLC formation costs $300** with 2–3 day processing via SOSDirect. The annual franchise tax report is required by May 15 but **no tax is due below $2.47M in revenue** — only a Public Information Report must be filed. For out-of-state software sales, **foreign LLC registration is generally not required** for interstate commerce alone; it triggers only with physical office, employees, or warehouse presence in another state.

**Sales tax nexus is the more pressing concern.** Since South Dakota v. Wayfair (2018), states can impose economic nexus at **$100,000 in sales or 200 transactions**. Texas treats SaaS as a taxable data processing service at **up to 8.25%** with a 20% exemption. **24 states now tax SaaS** in some form. A compliance automation service (TaxJar, Avalara, Anrok at $50–$500/mo) is essential once selling across state lines.

The **EULA must include robust financial disclaimers.** The software must clearly state it does not constitute financial, investment, tax, or legal advice. Projections should be labeled as hypothetical estimates. Key language: *"Results are estimates only and should not be relied upon as the sole basis for financial decisions. Consult qualified professionals."* Under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, providing personalized financial advice for compensation without registration carries **$5,000 per client penalties and up to 5 years imprisonment** — but tools providing generic projections based on user inputs are generally classified as analytical tools, not advisory services.

For **QuickBooks Online integration**, OAuth 2.0 with HTTPS redirect URIs is mandatory. Desktop apps need a lightweight cloud callback endpoint. The free Builder tier (500K CorePlus API calls/month) is sufficient to start. For **QuickBooks Desktop integration**, the SOAP/qbXML Web Connector requires **no Intuit approval** — significantly easier for a self-hosted product. The new webhook migration to CloudEvents format has a **May 15, 2026 deadline**.

**SOC 2 compliance is not legally required but increasingly expected** in B2B sales. For a self-hosted product where data stays on the customer's machine, SOC 2 scope is dramatically reduced compared to cloud SaaS. If TanoBox adds any cloud component (license validation, OAuth relay), those systems fall in scope. Cost is **$20,000–$100,000** for a full audit, but compliance automation platforms (Vanta, Drata) reduce this to **$10,000–$50,000/year**.

TanoBox occupies a market position that no competitor currently holds: **perpetual-license, self-hosted, AEC-focused 3-statement financial modeling** integrated with QuickBooks. The research reveals five structural advantages. First, the AEC vertical is genuinely underserved — 75% of architecture firms have fewer than 10 employees and most rely on QuickBooks plus Excel. Second, every FP&A competitor is subscription-based and industry-agnostic, making the one-time-purchase model a real differentiator at a moment when anti-SaaS sentiment is peaking. Third, the hardware-appliance delivery model has proven precedents generating hundreds of millions in revenue (Synology, Netgate). Fourth, the QuickBooks ecosystem has a measurable 3-statement modeling gap with fragmented, partial solutions and no dominant winner. Fifth, the AIA's concentrated, accessible membership structure (101,000 members, 200+ chapters, 90% renewal rate) provides an unusually efficient customer acquisition pathway for a solo founder.

The most actionable next steps are: price the software-only version at **$499–$799** and the hardware bundle at **$799–$1,299**, targeting the 1–2 year SaaS-savings payback threshold. Build QuickBooks Desktop integration first (no approval needed, SOAP-based) as the fastest path to market. Begin LinkedIn outreach to small AEC firm principals at ~30–45 personalized invitations per day. Sponsor one AIA local chapter ($2,500–$5,000) to test conference-adjacent acquisition. And use `formula.js` + `decimal.js` + a custom DAG engine as the technical foundation, solving circular references through iterative convergence.
